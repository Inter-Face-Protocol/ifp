## About

Pete initiated the Inter-Face project in February 2026, starting from a simple observation: he had friends doing interesting things, and they had friends doing interesting things, and none of them knew about each other's work because keeping up is expensive.

He wrote the original Inter-Face manifesto, drafted the first IFP (then a combined philosophy and message format document), and has been collaborating with Victoria Tanner and several Claude instances to develop the specification series.

## Contributions

## Perspective

Pete's approach to Inter-Face is shaped by direct experience with the IETF RFC process in the 1990s, two decades of work in collaborative technology and knowledge management, and a conviction that the protocols we build now for agent-to-agent communication will shape the social fabric of the next era of the internet.

He believes in rough consensus and running code, in specifications that are human-legible and implementable in a few hundred lines, and in the principle that protocols exist to serve people.

## Why the Rush

I'm building this as fast as I can, in service of the world. I know it's imperfect. I offer it anyway.

The reasoning is simple: we have 60-plus years of protocol design history to draw on, and we now have agentic AI that changes the fundamental working assumptions behind much of that history. Some of the core principles that everyone "knows" about doing protocols right -- Postel's Law, authenticate-before-communicate, strict layer isolation -- need to be recast for a world of fast, reasoning agents. So we're recasting them.

We're speed-running a review of those six decades of protocol stacks, building an open knowledge base of everything we and anyone else can contribute about how protocols have worked and what's changed, and using all of that to build an agent-mediated communication network as quickly as we responsibly can.

We'll get parts wrong. That's why we're using the IETF RFC / BIP / NIP rough-and-ready collaborative standards development process -- it's designed for imperfect starts and iterative improvement. The specifications earn adoption through usefulness, not through being perfect on the first try.

The invitation is genuine and unconditional: join us. And if you've got something better -- if you're running faster than we are and you're meeting the requirements for decentralization, privacy, human legibility, and human agency -- then we'll join you. Either way, we end up working together to make this right.
